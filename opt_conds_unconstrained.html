Conditions of optimality

Here we consider two types of conditions of optimality for unconstrained minimization problems: necessary and sufficient conditions. We show the necessary conditions of the first and second order, while the sufficient condition only of the second order. Necessary conditions must be satisfied at the minimum, but even when they are, the optimality is not guaranteed. On the other hand, the sufficient conditions need not be satisfied at the minimum, but if they are, the optimality is guaranteed.

Scalar optimization variable

We recall here the fundamental assumption made at the beginning of our introduction to optimization – we only consider optimization variables x that are real-valued (first, just scalar \x \in \mathbb R, later vectors \bm x \in \mathbb R^n), and objective functions f() that are sufficiently smooth – all the derivatives exist. The conditions of optimality can be derived upon looking that the Taylor series approximation of the cost function around the minimum.

Taylor series approximation around the optimum

Denote x^* as the (local) minimum of the function f(x). The Taylor series expansion of f(x) around x^* is

f(x^*+\alpha) = f(x^*)+f'(x^*)\alpha + \frac{1}{2}f''(x^*)\alpha^2 + {\color{blue}\mathcal{O}(\alpha^3)}, where \mathcal{O}(\alpha^3) is called Big-O and is defined as \lim_{\alpha\rightarrow 0}\frac{\mathcal{O}(\alpha^3)}{\alpha^3} \leq M<\infty,

Alternatively, we can write the Taylor series expansion as

f(x^*+\alpha) = f(x^*)+f'(x^*)\alpha + \frac{1}{2}f''(x^*)\alpha^2 + {\color{red}o(\alpha^2)}, using the little-o defined as \lim_{\alpha\rightarrow 0}\frac{o(\alpha^2)}{\alpha^2} = 0

First-order necessary conditions of optimality

\frac{\mathrm{d}f(x)}{\mathrm{d} x} = 0.

Second-order necessary conditions of optimality

\frac{\mathrm{d^2}f(x)}{\mathrm{d} x^2} \geq 0.

Vector optimization variable

\nabla f(\mathbf x) = \mathbf 0, where the gradient as a column vector \nabla f(\mathbf x) := \begin{bmatrix}\frac{\partial f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_1} \\ \frac{\partial f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_n} \\ \vdots \\ \frac{\partial f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_n}\end{bmatrix}.

Example: for f(\mathbf x) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{r}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{x}, the gradient is \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{r} and the first-order condition of optimality is \mathbf{Q}\mathbf{x} = -\mathbf{r}.

Second-order sufficient condition of optimality

Scalar optimization variable

\frac{\mathrm{d^2}f(x)}{\mathrm{d} x^2} > 0.

Second-order sufficient condition of optimality

\frac{\mathrm{d^2}f(x)}{\mathrm{d} x^2} > 0.

Vector optimization variable

Second-order necessary conditions of optimality

\nabla^2 f(\mathbf x) \succeq 0, where \nabla^2 f(\mathbf x) is the Hessian (the symmetrix matrix of the second-order mixed partial derivatives) \nabla^2 f(x) = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_1^2} && \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_1\partial x_2} && \ldots && \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_1\partial x_n}\\ \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_2\partial x_1} && \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_2^2} && \ldots && \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_2\partial x_n}\\ \vdots\\ \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_n\partial x_1} && \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_n\partial x_2} && \ldots && \frac{\partial^2 f(\mathbf x)}{\partial x_n\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix} and the interpretation of the inequality (\succeq) is not elementwise but in terms of eigenvalues (the matrix is positive semidefinite).

Example 1 For the quadratic function f(\mathbf x) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{r}^\mathrm{T}\mathbf{x}, the Hessian is \nabla^2 f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{Q} and the second-order necessary condition of optimality is \mathbf{Q} \succeq 0.

Second-order sufficient condition of optimality

\nabla^2 f(\mathbf x) > 0, that is, the Hessian is positive definite.
